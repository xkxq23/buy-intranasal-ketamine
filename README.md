## What Is Intranasal Ketamine?

Intranasal ketamine refers to a form of ketamine administered through the nasal passages, often as a spray or powder. Ketamine, originally developed as an anesthetic in the 1960s, has gained attention for its potential in treating conditions like depression, chronic pain, and PTSD. Unlike traditional antidepressants, it works rapidly by targeting glutamate receptors in the brain.

In medical settings, intranasal ketamine is used under strict supervision, such as in FDA-approved formulations like esketamine (Spravato) for treatment-resistant depression. However, non-medical use raises concerns about addiction, dissociation, and long-term cognitive effects.

## Medical Uses and Benefits of Intranasal Ketamine

### Therapeutic Applications
- **Depression Treatment:** Studies show intranasal ketamine can alleviate symptoms in hours, compared to weeks for SSRIs. It's particularly effective for suicidal ideation.
- **Pain Management:** Used for neuropathic pain and complex regional pain syndrome due to its NMDA receptor blockade.
- **Anesthesia and Sedation:** In emergency medicine, it's valued for quick onset without respiratory depression.

### How It Works
Ketamine induces a dissociative state, blocking pain signals and promoting neuroplasticity. Intranasal delivery allows for faster absorption via the nasal mucosa, bypassing the digestive system for higher bioavailability (around 30-50%).

Research from institutions like Yale and Johns Hopkins highlights its promise, but ongoing trials emphasize the need for controlled dosing to avoid side effects like elevated blood pressure or hallucinations.

## Legal Considerations for Buying Intranasal Ketamine

Ketamine is classified as a Schedule III controlled substance in the US under the DEA, meaning it has accepted medical uses but high abuse potential. In the EU, it's similarly regulated. Legitimate purchases require a prescription from a qualified provider.

Unauthorized buying or selling can result in fines, imprisonment, or health complications from impure sources. Always verify sources through pharmacies or clinics. For international readers, check local laws—e.g., in Canada, it's Schedule I for non-medical use.
